SCIATICA FLARE-UP PROTOCOL
============================

Flares happen. They do not mean you failed or that the plan is broken.
They mean your nerve is irritated and you need to temporarily shift gears.
The goal during a flare is to maintain as much of the routine as possible
without making the flare worse.


SEVERITY LEVELS
=================

MILD: Increased back tightness or dull ache. No radiating leg pain.
  You can still function but you feel it.

MODERATE: Radiating pain into the glute or down the leg. Walking is
  uncomfortable. Sitting for long periods makes it worse.

SEVERE: Sharp, constant radiating pain. Difficulty walking or standing.
  Numbness or tingling in the foot. Significant activity limitation.


WHAT TO DO BY SEVERITY
========================

MILD FLARE:
  Keep doing:
    - Walking (reduce duration by 5-10 min, stay on flat ground)
    - McGill Big 3 (check form — are you doing them correctly?)
    - Hip stretches (piriformis stretch may directly help)
    - Resistance exercises (skip chair squats if they aggravate)
    - Meditation, diet, sleep, CBT logging — all continue normally

  Add:
    - Standing back extensions: hands on hips, gently lean back,
      hold 5 seconds, repeat 5x. Do these 3-4 times per day.
    - Ice on the affected area for 15 min after activity
    - Extra piriformis and hip flexor stretching (2-3x per day)

  Duration: Usually resolves in 2-5 days if managed.


MODERATE FLARE:
  Keep doing:
    - Walking (shorten to 5-10 min only, or switch to gentle movement
      around the house if outdoor walking hurts)
    - McGill Big 3 (only if they don't increase radiating pain —
      skip any exercise that makes the leg symptoms worse)
    - Piriformis stretch and hip flexor stretch (gently)
    - Glute bridges (often still tolerable and helpful)
    - Meditation (especially important — pain increases anxiety
      which increases pain perception)
    - Diet, sleep, CBT logging — all continue normally

  Pause:
    - Resistance exercises (wall push-ups, chair squats, band rows)
    - Pool exercise (getting in/out may aggravate)
    - Any exercise that increases the radiating pain

  Add:
    - Standing back extensions: 5 reps, 5-6 times per day
    - Alternate ice (15 min) and heat (15 min) on lower back
    - Change positions frequently — do not sit for more than 20-30 min
    - Walk gently for 2-3 min every 30 minutes of sitting
    - OTC anti-inflammatory (ibuprofen) per label directions if tolerated

  Duration: Usually 1-2 weeks. If not improving after 1 week, see your doctor.


SEVERE FLARE:
  Keep doing:
    - Meditation (this is when you need it most)
    - Diet (do not let pain become an excuse for emotional eating —
      use the CBT tools)
    - Sleep schedule (pain disrupts sleep; good sleep hygiene matters
      even more now)
    - CBT logging (especially: "pain is a trigger for eating" —
      notice and log it)
    - Hydration

  Pause:
    - Walking beyond basic daily movement
    - All exercises except gentle piriformis stretch if tolerated
    - Pool exercise

  Add:
    - Standing back extensions if tolerated (try cautiously)
    - Lying on your back with calves on a chair seat (90-90 position)
      for 10-15 min several times a day — this unloads the spine
    - Position changes every 20 minutes
    - OTC anti-inflammatory per label directions

  See your doctor if:
    - Pain is not improving after 2 weeks
    - You have numbness or weakness in your foot or lower leg
    - You have difficulty controlling your bladder or bowels (this is
      an EMERGENCY — go to the ER immediately, this is cauda equina
      syndrome)
    - Pain is so severe you cannot sleep at all despite medication

  Duration: Can take 2-4 weeks. Medical guidance recommended.


RESUMING THE PLAN AFTER A FLARE
=================================

Do NOT restart from week 1. Drop back in your progression and rebuild.

  If the flare lasted less than 1 week:
    - Resume where you left off but at the previous week's intensity
    - Example: you were on week 6 walking plan -> resume at week 5 levels
    - Return to your current week within 3-5 days if feeling good

  If the flare lasted 1-2 weeks:
    - Drop back 2 weeks in all progressions
    - Rebuild over 1-2 weeks back to where you were

  If the flare lasted more than 2 weeks:
    - Drop back 3-4 weeks in all progressions
    - Rebuild gradually — do not rush
    - Consider seeing a physical therapist before resuming

  General rules for resuming:
    - Start with walking before adding exercises back
    - Add McGill Big 3 and hip stretches next
    - Add resistance exercises last
    - If any exercise brings back radiating symptoms, stop it and wait
      another few days before trying again
    - Progress should feel easy at first — that is intentional


PREVENTING FUTURE FLARES
==========================
As you get stronger and lighter, flares should become less frequent and
less severe. The things that reduce flare risk long-term:

  - Consistent McGill Big 3 (core endurance protects the spine)
  - Strong glutes (glute bridges and clamshells take load off the spine)
  - Flexible hip flexors and piriformis (reduces nerve compression)
  - Lower body weight (less spinal compression)
  - Good sleep (reduces pain sensitivity)
  - Movement throughout the day (do not sit for hours without moving)
  - Managing stress and anxiety (tension increases muscle guarding
    around the spine, which can compress nerves)

These are all things you are already working on. Every week of consistency
reduces your flare risk.


WHAT NOT TO DO DURING A FLARE
================================
- Do NOT stop everything and lie in bed for days (prolonged rest makes
  sciatica worse after the first 24-48 hours)
- Do NOT push through sharp radiating pain hoping it will loosen up
- Do NOT abandon your diet because "nothing matters anyway" — this is
  the all-or-nothing thinking trap from the CBT guide
- Do NOT restart the entire plan from scratch — you haven't lost your
  progress, you just need to rebuild briefly
- Do NOT try to stretch or massage the pain away aggressively
- Do NOT ignore severe symptoms (numbness, weakness, bladder issues)
