CBT GUIDE FOR ANXIETY-DRIVEN EATING
======================================

Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT) is the most studied and effective
psychological approach for changing eating patterns tied to emotions.
The core idea: your thoughts drive your feelings, and your feelings
drive your behavior. Change the thoughts, change the behavior.

This is a self-guided version. If you find it helpful but want to go
deeper, a few sessions with a CBT therapist can accelerate the process
significantly.


THE ANXIETY-EATING CYCLE
==========================

  Trigger (stress, boredom, worry, pain)
       |
  Anxious thought ("I can't handle this", "I deserve a break")
       |
  Uncomfortable feeling (tension, restlessness, dread)
       |
  Urge to eat (seeking comfort/distraction/numbing)
       |
  Eat (temporary relief)
       |
  Guilt/frustration (which becomes the next trigger)
       |
  Repeat

The goal is not to eliminate anxiety. The goal is to break the chain
between the feeling and the eating.


STEP 1: AWARENESS — THE FOOD/MOOD LOG
=======================================

For the first 2 weeks, simply notice and write down. No changes needed yet.
Awareness alone reduces the behavior. Use a notebook, phone notes, whatever
is easiest.

Every time you eat outside of a planned meal, write:

  Time:       ___________
  What I ate: ___________
  Hunger?     Physical hunger (stomach growling) or emotional (just want it)?
  Feeling:    What was I feeling right before? (anxious, bored, stressed,
              tired, frustrated, lonely, in pain)
  Trigger:    What happened in the 30 min before? (argument, work stress,
              bad news, nothing specific, pain flare)
  After:      How do I feel now?

You are not judging yourself. You are collecting data. After 2 weeks you
will see clear patterns — specific feelings and situations that trigger eating.


STEP 2: CATCH THE THOUGHT
===========================

Anxiety-driven eating is powered by automatic thoughts. These are fast,
habitual, and feel like facts. They are not facts.

Common ones:
  "I need something to take the edge off."
  "I've had a hard day, I deserve this."
  "One won't hurt."
  "I'll start fresh tomorrow."
  "I can't relax without eating something."
  "Screw it, what's the point."

When you notice an urge to eat outside a planned meal, pause and ask:
  "What thought just went through my head?"

Write it down. That is the thought driving the behavior.


STEP 3: CHALLENGE THE THOUGHT
===============================

Once you catch the thought, run it through these questions:

  1. Is this actually true, or does it just feel true?
  2. What would I tell a friend who said this to me?
  3. Will eating actually solve the thing I'm anxious about?
  4. How will I feel 30 minutes after eating this?
  5. What is the feeling I'm actually trying to fix right now?

Then reframe the thought:

  Old: "I need something to take the edge off."
  New: "I'm feeling anxious. Food won't fix the anxiety. It will be
       gone in 20 minutes whether I eat or not."

  Old: "I deserve this after today."
  New: "I deserve to feel good tomorrow morning. Eating this won't
       help with that."

  Old: "Screw it, what's the point."
  New: "That's frustration talking, not logic. One good choice right
       now matters."

You do not need to believe the new thought perfectly. You just need to
say it and sit with it.


STEP 4: RIDE THE WAVE (URGE SURFING)
======================================

An eating urge is like a wave. It builds, peaks, and passes. It will not
last forever. Most urges pass within 10-20 minutes if you do not feed them.

When an urge hits:

  1. Notice it: "I'm having an urge to eat. That's fine. It's just a feeling."
  2. Rate it: 1-10, how strong is it?
  3. Set a timer for 15 minutes.
  4. Do one of these instead:
     - Walk outside (even 5 minutes)
     - Drink a full glass of water
     - Do your breathing exercise (see meditation plan)
     - Call or text someone
     - Do a small task (dishes, laundry, organize something)
  5. When the timer goes off, rate the urge again. It will almost always
     be lower.
  6. If you still want to eat after 15 minutes and honest reflection,
     eat — but slowly and intentionally, not standing at the counter.

Every time you ride out an urge, you weaken the habit loop. This is
where the real change happens.


STEP 5: BUILD ALTERNATIVE RESPONSES
=====================================

Anxiety needs an outlet. If you remove eating, you need to give it
somewhere else to go. Match the replacement to the actual need:

  If the need is comfort:    warm shower, cup of tea, call a friend
  If the need is distraction: walk, podcast, puzzle, small project
  If the need is numbing:     this is the hardest one — sit with the
                              feeling using meditation (see meditation plan)
  If the need is a reward:    plan non-food rewards (new music, a show
                              you're saving, 20 min doing something you enjoy)
  If the need is boredom:     boredom is often anxiety in disguise —
                              check what you're actually feeling


COMMON CBT THINKING TRAPS
===========================

Learn to recognize these. They fuel anxiety and emotional eating.

All-or-nothing thinking:
  "I ate a cookie so the whole day is ruined."
  Reality: One cookie is 200 calories. The day is fine.

Catastrophizing:
  "I'll never be able to change."
  Reality: You are working on it right now.

Emotional reasoning:
  "I feel like a failure, so I must be one."
  Reality: Feelings are not facts.

Should statements:
  "I should be able to just stop."
  Reality: Decades of habit don't vanish overnight. Progress is the goal.

Permission giving:
  "I've been good all week."
  Reality: This is not a punishment/reward system. It's a lifestyle.


WEEKLY PRACTICE SCHEDULE
==========================

Week 1-2:   Food/mood log only. Just observe. No pressure to change.
Week 3-4:   Start catching automatic thoughts. Write them down.
Week 5-6:   Practice challenging and reframing thoughts.
Week 7-8:   Practice urge surfing. Delay eating urges by 15 minutes.
Week 9+:    Build and refine your alternative response toolkit.
Ongoing:    Daily meditation (see meditation plan).


ALCOHOL AND ANXIETY
======================

This deserves its own section. If you use alcohol to manage anxiety —
even casually ("I just need a drink to unwind") — be honest with yourself
about it.

Alcohol is a depressant that temporarily reduces anxiety. But it causes
rebound anxiety the next day (your nervous system compensates by ramping
UP anxiety chemicals). This creates a cycle:

  Anxiety -> drink -> temporary relief -> worse anxiety next day ->
  drink again -> worse anxiety...

It also:
- Destroys sleep quality (even if you fall asleep faster)
- Adds 150-300+ empty calories per drink
- Lowers inhibitions around food (drunk/buzzed eating)
- Worsens inflammation (more pain, more sciatica triggers)

What to do:
- Notice if you're reaching for a drink for the same reasons you reach
  for food: stress, boredom, anxiety, pain
- Use the same CBT tools: catch the thought, challenge it, ride the wave
- The 4-7-8 breathing technique works for alcohol urges too
- If you find you cannot cut back despite wanting to, tell your doctor.
  There are effective, low-stigma medical options. This is not a
  willpower problem.


REALISTIC EXPECTATIONS
========================
- You will not do this perfectly. That is not the goal.
- The goal is to eat emotionally less often, not never.
- Progress looks like: binge 5x a week -> 3x a week -> 1x a week ->
  occasionally. That is a massive win.
- Setbacks are data, not failures. When you slip, do the log:
  what was the trigger, the thought, the feeling? Now you know more.
- Be patient with yourself. You are rewiring decades of coping patterns.
