COMPARISON: THIS PROGRAM vs MEDITERRANEAN DIET

OVERVIEW
This program is a high-protein, anti-inflammatory plan designed for weight loss,
sciatica management, and breaking anxiety-driven emotional eating. The Mediterranean
diet is a traditional eating pattern from southern Europe emphasizing whole foods,
healthy fats, and plant-forward meals. Both are evidence-based but differ in goals
and emphasis.

=========================================================================
WHERE THEY AGREE (STRONG OVERLAP)
=========================================================================

Extra virgin olive oil as primary cooking fat
  Both: EVOO is central. Anti-inflammatory, rich in monounsaturated fats.

Fatty fish 2-3x per week
  Both: Salmon, sardines, mackerel for omega-3 fatty acids.

Abundant vegetables daily
  Both: Colorful vegetables, leafy greens, cruciferous vegetables.

Legumes as a staple
  Both: Lentils, chickpeas, black beans appear frequently.

Nuts in moderation
  Both: Walnuts, almonds. This program caps at 1/4 cup/day.

Avoid processed foods
  Both: No processed meats, fried foods, excess sugar, seed oils.

Anti-inflammatory spices
  Both: Turmeric, ginger, garlic, herbs.

Eggs included
  Both: Regular egg consumption (this program 7-14/week, Med diet 2-4/week).

Greek yogurt and cheese
  Both: Fermented dairy in moderate portions (feta, Greek yogurt).

Blueberries and fruit
  Both: Berries and whole fruits encouraged.

=========================================================================
WHERE THEY DIFFER
=========================================================================

PROTEIN TARGETS
  This program: 130-150g/day (30g+ per meal). Protein is the anchor of
  every meal for satiety and muscle preservation during weight loss.
  Mediterranean: ~15-20% of calories (~60-80g/day typical). Protein is
  present but not the organizing principle.
  VERDICT: This program is significantly higher protein. Mediterranean
  dieters focused on weight loss or over-50 muscle preservation may need
  to intentionally increase protein beyond traditional Med patterns.

WHOLE GRAINS
  This program: Minimal grains. Starches come from lentils, beans, sweet
  potatoes. No bread, pasta, rice, or wheat-based foods on the menu.
  Mediterranean: Whole grains are a foundation -- whole-wheat bread, pasta,
  farro, bulgur, brown rice, oats appear daily.
  VERDICT: Major difference. Mediterranean diet treats whole grains as a
  primary food group. This program replaces them with legumes and tubers
  to keep protein high and manage caloric density.

RED MEAT
  This program: Burger (ground beef) appears 2x/week on the menu.
  Mediterranean: Red meat is occasional at most (1x/week or less).
  VERDICT: This program includes more red meat than a traditional
  Mediterranean pattern. Consider substituting one burger night with
  fish or legumes to align more closely.

ALCOHOL / WINE
  This program: Alcohol completely avoided.
  Mediterranean: Moderate red wine with meals is traditional (1 glass/day).
  VERDICT: This program's zero-alcohol rule is stricter, appropriate for
  someone managing anxiety and emotional eating triggers.

CALORIE COUNTING / STRUCTURE
  This program: Precise gram-level protein tracking, structured meal timing,
  kitchen-closes-at-8-PM rule. Highly structured.
  Mediterranean: No calorie counting. Emphasizes food quality and intuitive
  eating. Meals are social, relaxed occasions.
  VERDICT: Fundamentally different approaches. This program uses structure
  to combat emotional eating. Mediterranean relies on food quality and
  mindfulness to naturally regulate intake.

FAT INTAKE
  This program: Fats limited (1 tbsp olive oil, 1/4 cup nuts max).
  Mediterranean: Generous healthy fat intake (30-35% of calories). EVOO
  used liberally on salads, bread, cooking.
  VERDICT: This program restricts fat more aggressively for caloric
  deficit. Mediterranean diet embraces healthy fats as satiating and
  heart-protective.

FRUIT VARIETY
  This program: Mostly blueberries (1/2 cup daily), occasional apple.
  Mediterranean: Wide fruit variety -- figs, citrus, pomegranates, grapes,
  melons, stone fruits eaten daily and seasonally.
  VERDICT: Mediterranean offers more fruit diversity. This program limits
  fruit for calorie control but could benefit from more variety.

DAIRY AMOUNT
  This program: Heavy dairy (Greek yogurt, cottage cheese, string cheese
  multiple times daily) as a primary protein source.
  Mediterranean: Moderate dairy, mostly fermented (yogurt, aged cheese).
  Dairy is a complement, not a protein vehicle.
  VERDICT: This program uses substantially more dairy to hit protein
  targets.

=========================================================================
WHAT THIS PROGRAM COULD ADOPT FROM MEDITERRANEAN
=========================================================================

1. More fruit variety -- add seasonal fruits beyond blueberries
2. Replace one burger night with grilled fish or legume stew
3. Add whole grains occasionally (farro, bulgur) if protein targets allow
4. Use EVOO more liberally on salads and vegetables for flavor and satiety
5. Embrace the social/mindful eating philosophy -- eat slowly, enjoy meals

=========================================================================
WHAT MEDITERRANEAN COULD LEARN FROM THIS PROGRAM
=========================================================================

1. Higher protein targets for adults over 50 preserving muscle mass
2. Structured meal timing helps break emotional eating cycles
3. CBT-based tools for managing food-mood connections
4. Specific anti-inflammatory protocols for pain conditions (sciatica)
5. Supplement guidance (D3, magnesium, omega-3) backed by citations

=========================================================================
BOTTOM LINE
=========================================================================

This program is already ~70% Mediterranean in its food choices: olive oil,
fish, vegetables, legumes, nuts, and fermented dairy. The key departures --
higher protein, less grains, more structure, no alcohol -- are intentional
adaptations for weight loss, muscle preservation, sciatica management, and
anxiety-eating control in an adult over 50. These are reasonable, evidence-
based modifications to Mediterranean principles rather than contradictions.

The two approaches are more complementary than competitive.
