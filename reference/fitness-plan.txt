FITNESS PLAN — SCIENCE-BACKED RECOVERY PROGRAM
================================================

Profile: 57-year-old male, 6ft, 235lbs
Conditions: Back and hip issues, sciatica triggered by spinal impact/load bearing
Starting fitness: Deconditioned, gets winded quickly
Date created: 2026-02-24


1. WALKING — DAILY, PROGRESSIVE
--------------------------------
The single most studied intervention for deconditioned adults and first-line
treatment for sciatica in clinical guidelines. Walking promotes disc nutrition
and reduces nerve irritation.

Protocol:
- Start with whatever duration you can manage without triggering sciatica (even 10 min)
- Add 2-5 minutes per week
- Target: 30-45 minutes most days
- Flat, even surfaces preferred to start

See walking-plan.txt for the full 12-week progression.

Why it matters: Consistently shown to reduce all-cause mortality more than any
other single behavior change.


2. CALORIC DEFICIT VIA PROTEIN-PRIORITIZED DIET
-------------------------------------------------
At 6ft/235lbs, losing 20-30lbs will dramatically reduce spinal load and
sciatica flares.

Protocol:
- Aim for 130-150g protein per day (minimum 30g per meal for muscle preservation)
- Ramp protein gradually over 2-3 weeks (don't jump to 150g on day one)
- High protein preserves muscle during weight loss and has the highest satiety per calorie
- Research shows adults 50+ need 30g minimum per meal to maximize muscle protein synthesis
- Cut liquid calories (soda, juice, alcohol) — this alone often creates a meaningful deficit
- Protein + vegetables at every meal + modest portions
- Anti-inflammatory foods (fatty fish, olive oil, colorful vegetables) help with pain
- Track food for 5 days at the start to calibrate your portion sense

See eating-guide.txt for meals, grocery list, and details.

Why it matters: Every pound lost reduces spinal compression. High protein
prevents muscle loss during the deficit.


3. AQUATIC EXERCISE (POOL WALKING / SWIMMING)
----------------------------------------------
Highest-value exercise modality for sciatica. Water removes ~90% of body
weight load from the spine.

Protocol:
- Pool walking, water aerobics, or swimming
- Start with 2x per week, 20-30 minutes
- Progress duration and intensity as tolerated

Why it matters: Research shows aquatic exercise improves cardiovascular fitness,
reduces pain, and builds strength in people with chronic low back pain at rates
comparable to land-based exercise — without the flares.


4. CORE STABILITY + HIP STRENGTH + RESISTANCE TRAINING
--------------------------------------------------------
Three categories of exercise, all spine-safe:

Core stability (McGill Big 3):
  - Curl-up, side plank, bird-dog
  - Builds muscular endurance around the spine that protects against sciatica

Hip exercises:
  - Glute bridges, clamshells, hip flexor stretch, piriformis stretch
  - Directly addresses hip pain and common sciatica contributors
  - Hip flexor tightness and glute weakness are among the most common
    drivers of both conditions

Basic resistance training:
  - Wall push-ups, chair-assisted squats, resistance band rows
  - Preserves muscle mass during weight loss (critical at 57 — sarcopenia
    is already a concern at this age)
  - Without resistance training, a significant portion of weight lost will
    be muscle, leaving you lighter but still weak

See core-exercises.txt for all exercise descriptions, form cues, and progressions.

Why it matters: Addresses your spine, your hips, and age-related muscle loss
in one combined program. 15-20 minutes per session.


5. CONSISTENT SLEEP (7-8 HOURS)
---------------------------------
Heavily supported by data. Poor sleep independently:
- Increases pain sensitivity (lowers sciatica threshold)
- Impairs recovery from exercise
- Drives hunger hormones (ghrelin up, leptin down), sabotaging weight loss
- Reduces exercise motivation and capacity

Protocol:
- Target 7-8 hours per night
- Keep consistent bed and wake times (consistency matters more than total hours)
- Limit screens 30-60 min before bed

See sleep-plan.txt for the full protocol, bedroom setup, and troubleshooting.

Why it matters: Fixing sleep amplifies the returns on every other intervention.


IMPLEMENTATION SCHEDULE
========================
Week 1       Walking + diet changes + sleep schedule + meditation + food/mood log
Week 2       Continue above, add kitchen-closes rule
Week 3       Add core + hip exercises (Routine A, 3x/week)
Week 4       Add resistance exercises (Routine B, 2x/week)
Week 5+      Add pool/aquatic exercise (2x/week)
Week 6+      Full routine, CBT techniques fully active

See daily-schedule.txt for how this all fits into a single day.


LONG-TERM VIEW
================
This plan covers roughly the first 12 weeks. Here is what comes after:

Months 3-6:
- Walking is now a habit at 30-45 min, 5-6x/week
- Progress exercise variations (counter push-ups, deeper squats, heavier bands)
- Weight loss continues; reassess calorie needs if progress stalls
- Consider adding light dumbbell work if spine and hips tolerate it
- CBT skills are becoming automatic; emotional eating episodes decreasing

Months 6-12:
- Explore new activities your lighter, stronger body can handle
  (hiking, cycling, gym machines, yoga)
- Resistance training can progress to a simple full gym program if desired
- Reassess with your doctor — bloodwork, sciatica status, medication review
- Weight should be approaching goal range; shift to maintenance calories
- The habits are now your lifestyle, not a "program"

Beyond 12 months:
- Maintain what you've built
- The goal shifts from weight loss to strength, mobility, and quality of life
- Annual checkups with your doctor to monitor progress


FLARE-UP PROTOCOL
==================
Sciatica flares happen. A flare is not a reason to quit — it's a reason
to shift gears temporarily. See flare-up-protocol.txt for the full plan.

Quick reference:
- KEEP doing: meditation, diet, sleep schedule, CBT logging, hydration
- MODIFY: walking (shorten or pause), exercises (stop if they aggravate)
- RESUME: gradually, dropping back 1-2 weeks in your progression
- SEE A DOCTOR if: flare lasts longer than 2 weeks, numbness/weakness
  in foot or leg, or bladder/bowel issues (emergency)


IMPORTANT NOTES
================
- Get medical clearance before starting, especially given sciatica.
- Consider 2-3 physical therapy sessions to identify your specific sciatica
  triggers and get personalized modifications.
- Listen to your body — sharp or radiating pain is a stop signal, not something
  to push through.
- Track your weight weekly (same time, same conditions) rather than daily to
  see the real trend.
- Tell one person what you're doing. Accountability improves adherence
  significantly. See daily-schedule.txt for options.
